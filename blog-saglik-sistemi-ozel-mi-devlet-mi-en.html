    Is It Possible to Leave the Healthcare System to the Private Sector?

    Whether the healthcare system can be left to the private sector is a debate with not only economic but also ethical, legal, and social dimensions. Neoliberal and anarcho-capitalist schools of thought tend to favor private-sector-heavy models, while social democratic, social liberal, and various left-wing views argue that the public sector must play a strong role. For this reason, evaluating this issue raises a humanitarian question that goes beyond the debate over economic efficiency. Looking at specific countries, Cuba is one of the countries where healthcare is most accessible and where the system functions best overall. However, due to the embargoes currently in place, access to advanced medical technology is limited. In the exact opposite case, in the United States, the fact that the healthcare system is largely private and the state's role in it is very limited pushes technology and efficiency to their maximum levels, while creating a huge gap in access to healthcare between the wealthy and everyone else.

    So let's get to our main question: can the healthcare system really be left entirely to the private sector? For instance, the United States imposes no regulation on the price or profit margin at which cancer drugs are sold. This brings to mind a question attributed to former Turkish Prime Minister Süleyman Demirel, one I'm also very fond of: "Does an industry that sells medicine actually want health?" It might sound to some like an offhand remark, but there is a very deep truth behind it. In a fully private system like that of the United States, organizations with assets exceeding 5 billion dollars — such as the American Cancer Society, the H. Lee Moffitt Cancer Center, and Stowers Resource Management — support people who would otherwise struggle to access care. Yet even if we assume there are no victims who go unheard, what happens if, during an economic crisis, the donors behind these organizations reduce or completely stop their donations? This is not without historical precedent. During the Great Depression of 1929, for example, when the financial support of charities and privately-funded hospitals in the US was largely cut off, thousands of people were left to their fate because they could not access treatment. In other words, the most vulnerable segment of society became an indirect victim of nothing more than a stock market crash or an economic downturn.

    This raises the question of just how right it is to leave the system entirely free with no state intervention at all. It becomes even more telling when you consider reports that the US allocated a $1.5 trillion budget to military spending in 2026. Given the consequences of leaving the healthcare system entirely to the private sector, one can genuinely find oneself thinking, "Let's leave healthcare entirely to the state instead." It is fair to say that hybrid models have proven more successful worldwide than either fully private or fully state-run systems — at least, satisfaction surveys on healthcare systems across countries point in this direction. An important example is Taiwan, which has ranked first in "Best Healthcare in the World" rankings for seven years running. And yet, until 1995, nearly 40% of Taiwan's population had no health coverage at all. Afterward, a national health insurance system called National Health Insurance (NHI) was established, and thanks to this hybrid model, the country reached its current level of success in a relatively short period of time.

    The digital capacity of Taiwan's system closely resembles the E-Nabız system we have here in Turkey. Every citizen, as well as every foreign resident, has a chip-embedded NHI Health Card. Through this card, a doctor can access a cloud-based system called MediCloud and see, on a single screen, which hospital took which X-ray of yours in the past, which medications you've used, and your allergies. A similar system exists in Turkey as well — but unlike ours, it prevents the same procedures from being repeated over and over.

    Today, if you go to the emergency room closest to your home, you'll find a long line and far more people than you'd expect pressuring the doctor for an IV drip just because they have a headache. There are also plenty of people in Turkey who try to pressure doctors into prescribing medication or ordering unnecessary blood tests. The biggest burden this places on the system is that it clogs up appointments. It's clear that the E-Nabız system has digital infrastructure just as advanced as Taiwan's — but one of the main reasons it's used so heavily is that people abuse it, thinking "It's free anyway."

    For example, if you book an appointment right now and cancel it three hours before the scheduled time, nobody asks, "Why are you doing this?" As long as you cancelled, there's no issue. But when doing this repeatedly carries no consequence whatsoever, people stop taking the system seriously. Requesting an IV drip as casually as asking for a bottle of water at a kiosk, and having that request accepted without question, ends up harming the patients who genuinely need care.

    On top of making abuses like these impossible, Taiwan's administrative management cost is only around 2%. In Turkey, this figure is said to range between 10% and 27%. That means that, out of every 100 lira the state spends on healthcare, somewhere between 10 and 27 lira goes to administrative expenses. There can be many reasons for this — one being that hospitals' arguably unnecessary expenditures and surplus public staff place an additional burden on the system. Whether it's really necessary, in 2026, for hospitals to employ staff whose only job is to tell people "Second corridor on the right, internal medicine department," is a separate debate in itself. Instead, digital wayfinding systems (with audio options for people with disabilities), more directional signage, and a simpler hospital layout could largely solve this problem. However, the public-sector hiring that governments carry out around election periods can be seen as a form of populist policy, and this directly reduces the efficiency of state hospitals.

    The main reason Taiwan's system developed into one that is both efficient and strong is precisely that it chose a hybrid model free of these problems. You might be surprised, but roughly 80% of hospitals belong to the private sector. Yet the state has not left the system completely unregulated, the way the US has. When global pharmaceutical giants or medical equipment manufacturers want to enter the Taiwanese market, they find themselves facing a single counterpart: the Taiwanese state. Using its power as the sole buyer (monopsony), the state is able to negotiate and set the prices of medicines and medical supplies.

    The combination of the private sector's dynamism and efficiency with the state's regulatory power forms the foundation of the system's success. While only about 2% of the taxes paid by 24 million citizens is spent on operational activities, the remaining resources are channeled to private-sector hospitals and to medicine and medical supply providers. Running all of these processes through a digital, centralized system allows the state to benefit from the private sector's efficiency without leaving healthcare services at its mercy. Indeed, the success of this model is evident from the fact that it has ranked first in international healthcare system rankings for the past seven years, alongside high citizen satisfaction.

    In summary, when we think this through with the question "Does an industry that sells medicine actually want health?" at the center, we should recognize health as a fundamental human right and aim to build the most efficient, accessible, and sustainable system possible toward that end. With a structure that preserves the public sector's oversight role while putting the private sector's dynamism to work for the system, the hybrid model appears to be one of the strongest options for reaching that goal.
